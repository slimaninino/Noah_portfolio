Problem framing

Problem and Motivation

Why applying federated learning to Algerian dialect NLP is both challenging and worthwhile.

Challenge

  • Algerian dialect involves code-switching, Arabizi, mixed scripts, and heavy noise from social platforms.
  • Centralized training is effective but real institutions face hard privacy and governance constraints on sharing raw data.
  • Federated learning enables collaborative model training while keeping data local.

Research goal

  • Test whether DziriBERT retains strong performance when fine-tuned in a federated setting.
  • Compare FedAvg and FedProx on highly non-IID client distributions.
  • Quantify the trade-off between privacy-preserving training and accuracy loss.
Why this paper matters for real-world NLP deployment.

Experimental design

Dataset and Experimental Setup

How the data were collected, split, and distributed across simulated federated clients.

Dataset

  • 13,260 X-platform comments with balanced binary sentiment labels.
  • 54.2% Latin script, 38.4% Arabic script, 7.4% mixed.
  • Split: 70% train, 10% validation, 20% test.
  • Train split partitioned into 10 non-IID clients via Dirichlet α = 0.5.

Protocol

  • 15 global rounds; 10 clients total, 5 sampled per round.
  • 1 local epoch per round, batch size 8.
  • AdamW optimizer, learning rate 2e-5, gradient clipping 1.0.
  • Token length fixed to 128 across all methods.
What was trained, how it was partitioned, and how it was evaluated.

Baseline method

Why FedAvg?

FedAvg is the most interpretable starting point for any federated learning benchmark.

Why choose it

  • FedAvg is the canonical FL baseline—the most widely cited and easily reproduced reference point.
  • Simple to implement, it gives a fair and transparent baseline for future comparisons.
  • Starting with the standard algorithm is academically appropriate for a first benchmark study.

How it works

  • Server broadcasts the current global model to a sampled subset of clients.
  • Each client trains locally for one epoch on its own data partition.
  • Server aggregates client updates as a weighted average by local sample count.
  • Simple and efficient—but sensitive to client drift under non-IID distributions.

Robustness under heterogeneity

Why FedProx?

FedProx was selected to address the update drift risk that arises from severely imbalanced client data.

Why choose it

  • FedProx is a direct extension of FedAvg designed for heterogeneous, non-IID settings.
  • Client sizes ranged from 4 to 5,003 samples—making update drift a critical risk.
  • FedProx targets exactly that problem while keeping the same overall FL structure.

How it works

  • Adds a proximal penalty term to each client’s local loss function.
  • The penalty limits how far each client’s model drifts from the global model.
  • Result: more stable local updates and less divergence across heterogeneous clients.
  • Keeps local training useful without allowing clients to over-specialise.
The comparison method is stronger because it explicitly manages drift.

Hyperparameters

Why These Parameters?

The chosen values prioritise a credible first benchmark rather than aggressive tuning.

α = 0.5
Creates meaningfully heterogeneous non-IID clients rather than near-IID easy splits.
R = 15 rounds
Enough to show convergence trends without excessive compute cost.
m = 5 clients
50% participation rate balances realism with stable per-round aggregation.
LR = 2e-5
Standard safe fine-tuning rate for transformers to preserve pretrained weights.
μ = 0.01
Heuristic FedProx value: regularises drift without freezing local learning.

These are reasonable benchmark choices, not fully optimised hyperparameters. The paper explicitly identifies μ sweeps, α sweeps, and multi-seed evaluation as future work. The contribution is a strong first benchmark, not a final tuned system.

Main takeaway

  • Centralized training remains the strongest overall.
  • FedProx is the best-performing federated method in this study.
  • FedProx closes the gap to centralized training to < 1 percentage point.

Interpretation

  • FedAvg is a solid baseline but non-IID drift lowers its score.
  • FedProx’s proximal term actively stabilises training under heterogeneity.
  • SSFL shows pseudo-labelling is viable but costly when only 20% of clients are labelled.
FedProx comes closest to centralized performance in this benchmark.
